## Section 2: The Problem

**Header**: The solo agent ceiling

**Body**:
You've got Claude in one tab, Cursor in another, Copilot in your editor. Each one is impressive alone. But none of them coordinate. Nobody's watching architecture while someone else ships features. Nobody remembers what was decided yesterday. Nobody pushes back on a bad approach.

You're the glue. You relay context, catch conflicts, maintain coherence. It works — until it doesn't scale.

---

## Section 3: What Dax Is

**Header**: A team, not a tool

**Body**:
Dax is an open-source framework for orchestrating AI agents as a structured software team. Each agent has a defined role — product manager, designer, architect, implementor, delivery coordinator — with its own context, decision rights, and persistent memory. They communicate through a structured bridge, not ad-hoc prompting. You stay in the loop without being the loop.

## Section 4: How It Works

**Header**: The delivery loop

**Body** (intro): Here's what actually happens when Dax runs:

**Step 1**: Sisko + Ziyal design a bet — discover what's worth building through experiments.

**Step 2**: Kira composes slices with requirements and acceptance criteria.

**Step 3**: O'Brien and/or Leeta build it — backend, frontend, or both.

**Step 4**: Nog reviews the code — quality gates, standards, merge decision.

**Step 5**: Bashir checks acceptance criteria end-to-end.

**Step 6**: Kira accepts, amends, or rejects as the final instance.

**Annotation**: No human relay. No copy-pasting context between tools. The agents talk to each other.

## Section 5: Team Meetings

**Header**: Pull roles into a room. Let them argue.

**Body**:
The delivery loop is the default mode. But the best decisions happen when roles with different perspectives sit at the same table.

Call a team meeting with any combination of roles. You pick who's in the room and what the question is. They bring their own context, their own biases, their own blind spots — and they push back on each other.

**Example cards:**
- **Creative alignment** — Ziyal + Jake + Vic review a landing page draft. Does the design serve the copy? Does the copy match the brand?
- **Architecture risk** — Dax + O'Brien + Odo debate whether a new integration is worth the attack surface. Trade-offs surfaced before code is written.
- **Sprint planning** — Sisko + Kira + O'Brien scope the next slice. What's the bet? What's feasible?

**Closing line**: You're always in the room. You set the agenda, you hear the arguments, you make the call. The roles give you perspectives you'd otherwise have to hold in your own head.
